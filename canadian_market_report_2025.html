1. Canadian Economic Overview

GDP Growth

The Canadian economy has experienced notable volatility in 2025, heavily influenced by U.S. trade policy:

  • Q3 2025: GDP expanded at an annualized rate of 2.6%, surprising to the upside
  • October 2025: The economy contracted 0.3%, the largest monthly decline in nearly three years
  • November 2025: Preliminary data suggests a modest 0.1% rebound
  • Full Year Projection: The OECD projects GDP growth of approximately 1.0% for 2025, down from 1.5% in 2024

The economy borrowed growth in Q1 (2.2% annualized) as businesses rushed to get ahead of tariffs, followed by a sharp reversal. Canadian goods exports to the U.S. dropped more than 15% in April alone.

Inflation

Inflation has returned to target after the post-pandemic surge:

  • October 2025: CPI inflation slowed to 2.2%
  • Core Inflation: Remains in the 2.5% to 3% range, with underlying inflation around 2.5%
  • Outlook: The Bank of Canada expects inflation to remain around 2% over the projection horizon

Employment

The labour market has shown recent improvement:

  • November 2025: Unemployment rate declined to 6.5%
  • Recent Trend: After declining through the summer, employment has posted solid gains for three consecutive months
  • Tariff Impact: U.S. tariffs had near-immediate impacts on jobs in directly affected industries, particularly steel (23,000 jobs at risk) and aluminum (9,500 jobs at risk)

2. Bank of Canada Monetary Policy

Current Policy Stance

The Bank of Canada held its policy rate steady at 2.25% on December 10, 2025:

  • Bank Rate: 2.5%
  • Deposit Rate: 2.20%
  • Total Easing Since Peak: 275 basis points - more than any other G7 nation

Policy Rationale

Governor Tiff Macklem characterized the current rate as "about right" to keep inflation close to 2% while helping the economy through structural adjustment. Key factors supporting the hold:

  • Q3 GDP growth of 2.6% exceeded expectations
  • Labour market improvement with unemployment falling
  • Inflation returning to target
  • Need to maintain policy flexibility amid U.S. trade uncertainty

Forward Guidance

The Bank stands ready to respond if the outlook changes, but current guidance suggests no near-term moves. Some economists suggest the next move could actually be a hike in the second half of 2026 if inflation pressures emerge.

6. Key Sectors to Watch

Energy Sector

Strengths:

  • Canada remains 4th largest global crude oil producer, 5th largest natural gas producer
  • Pipeline throughput reached record highs above 4.60 million barrels per day
  • LNG Canada Phase 1 reached commercial operation in June 2025, exporting 1.9 Bcf/d

Challenges:

  • WTI prices have weakened; 2026 futures average approximately US$61/B vs. US$76/B in 2024
  • Up to 66% of future capital investments at risk of becoming stranded under 1.5C climate scenarios

Banking/Financial Sector

Performance:

  • Sector gained nearly 31% in 2025
  • All Big Six banks reporting strong earnings growth (14-29% revenue growth)
  • Mortgage renewal cycle managed better than feared

Key Players:

  • RBC: Largest by market cap ($298B), Q4 revenue up 14%, net income up 29%
  • TD: Adjusted revenue up 7.6%, net income up 22%
  • National Bank: Completed largest-ever acquisition (Canadian Western Bank)

Mining Sector

Gold:

  • Canada projected to produce over 200 metric tons in 2025 (top-5 globally)
  • Gold prices surpassed $3,000/oz for first time in March; now above $4,400/oz
  • Agnico Eagle posting record free cash flow, $1 billion net cash by June

Copper:

  • Prices stabilized above $9,000/tonne
  • Strategic importance for energy transition (EVs, renewables, grid infrastructure)
  • Major M&A: Lundin/BHP acquired Filo Corp for $4.1 billion

Technology Sector

Shopify:

  • Year-to-date gain of approximately 53%
  • Q3 revenue up 32% to US$2.8 billion
  • Black Friday-Cyber Monday sales hit record $14.6 billion
  • AI-driven traffic surged 7x; AI-driven orders up 11x

7. Risks and Opportunities

Key Risks

U.S. Trade Policy Uncertainty

25% tariffs on most Canadian goods remain in effect. Two million Canadian jobs rely on goods exports to the U.S. Trade-sensitive sectors (autos, steel, lumber) remain challenged.

Economic Growth Vulnerability

OECD projects only 1.0-1.1% growth through 2026. Exports and business investment expected to decline.

Real Estate Market Fragility

Elevated inventory levels with condo sector showing largest price declines. Ontario and BC markets still adjusting.

Opportunities

Diversification of Trade

Exports to Europe up 22%, Africa up 18%, Central/South America up 11%. LNG exports opening Asian markets.

Gold as Safe Haven

Record prices supporting mining sector profitability. New mine production coming online. Canadian gold producers well-positioned.

Critical Minerals Strategy

$4 billion federal strategy positions Canada as global supplier. Growing demand for copper in energy transition.
